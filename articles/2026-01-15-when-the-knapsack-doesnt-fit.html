Russianism, Martyrdom, and the Failure of Racial Essentialism

What happens when the framework of privilege can't account for the persecution of supposedly privileged groups?

By Evan Wologodzew ¬∑ January 15, 2026 ¬∑ 12 min read

In November 2025, U.S. Representative Joe Wilson accused clergy of the Russian Orthodox Church Outside Russia‚ÄîROCOR, including my bishop‚Äîof serving as "vehicles of foreign influence" for advocating religious freedom for the persecuted Ukrainian Orthodox Church.

The accusation is contemporary, but the pattern is ancient.

"Are you the King of the Jews?"

"My kingdom is not of this world."

Pontius Pilate could not compute an answer outside political categories. Representative Wilson cannot either. Neither could the Bolsheviks who executed my great-grandfather. The actors change, the language modernizes, the slogans rotate, but the mechanism remains: when the state encounters a reality it cannot categorize, it explains it materialistically. And once it has explained something in material terms, it can manage it. If it cannot manage it, it suppresses it.

What has changed is the moral vocabulary that provides cover. Peggy McIntosh's 1989 essay, White Privilege: Unpacking the Invisible Knapsack, has become administrative common sense across Western institutions. It presents itself as liberation. In practice, it functions as persecution infrastructure. It does not prevent state hostility toward religious minorities; it accelerates it, while insisting‚Äîoften sincerely‚Äîthat it is doing the opposite.

The Mechanism Across Time

The Church has met this dynamic in every century. Rome accused Christians of political subversion because they refused to worship the emperor. Later authorities condemned Orthodoxy as schism for refusing papal supremacy. Reformers dismissed ancient liturgy as superstition. Bolsheviks declared the Church a class enemy, branding Orthodoxy "Great Russian chauvinism." Today the charge is "white Christian nationalism."

Different words, same mechanism.

Unable to see communion as communion, the state reduces the Church to something it can count: ethnicity, race, geopolitics, influence, power. Once translated into those categories, the Church ceases to be sacrament and becomes threat. Wilson's accusation is not an aberration; it is the predictable output of a framework that cannot comprehend a Kingdom not of this world.

The Unfalsifiable Logic

McIntosh's framework claims that "Whiteness" confers an invisible package of unearned assets, among them the privilege of never having to speak for one's entire group. By that logic, what is happening to Orthodox Christians like me should be impossible. And yet here I am: treated not as a person but as a demographic abstraction, accused alongside my entire ecclesial community of collective loyalty to a foreign power, denied an individual voice precisely because I have been pre-sorted into a category.

This logic is effective as a tool of persecution because it is structured to be unfalsifiable. Older accusations required evidence. "You are a foreign agent" demands proof. "You possess invisible privilege" is designed so that denial confirms the charge. Self-defense becomes incriminating by definition; the framework makes acquittal impossible.

It also performs a moral inversion. The Bolsheviks knew they were destroying the Church. McIntosh-style frameworks can destroy while claiming to oppose oppression, recruiting the consciences of well-meaning people into machinery they cannot recognize as coercive. And it has achieved institutional totalization. It is no longer merely "the state." Universities, HR departments, NGOs, professional bureaucracies, and credentialing systems now operate with the same assumptions. There is no sanctuary outside it.

The structure is Puritan: inherited guilt without grace, collective condemnation without individual agency, sin without redemption. Worse, it universalizes the historical experience of an Anglo-American academic elite and projects it retroactively onto anyone with the "wrong" skin color. It cannot see the collapse of the Russian Empire, the Bolshevik annihilation of Orthodox elites, the exile that brought many of us here, or the persecution that continued long after. Where history should be visible, the framework projects "dominance." Once that projection is in place, accusations like Wilson's become not merely plausible but obligatory, a kind of moral hygiene.

A Heritage Written in Blood

I was baptized into ROCOR as an infant. My great-grandfather was executed by the Bolsheviks for loyalty to the Tsar and to the Orthodox faith. His body was discarded among countless others. I will never visit his grave.

I do not cite this to compete in oppression hierarchies. I cite it for pattern recognition. This has happened before. The justification changes; the logic does not.

The Bolsheviks did not target my family because they were marginalized. They targeted them because Orthodoxy was inseparable from an old imperial order‚Äîtoo rooted, too particular, too resistant to materialist translation. My great-grandfather was killed not as a "victim class" but as a perceived oppressor. My presence in America testifies to that annihilation. I am not here because my ancestors conquered new territory. I am here because they fled their homeland with nothing but their liturgy.

Watch the reasoning repeat across time. Bolshevik logic: you are oppressors of the people; therefore, execution is justice. McIntosh's logic: you possess invisible privilege; therefore, denial proves guilt. Wilson's logic: you are vehicles of foreign influence; therefore, your advocacy is suspect.

All three erase the Church as Church. All three replace spiritual reality with political category. All three justify suppression.

A Different Kind of Danger

This is not more violent than older persecutions. Rome fed Christians to lions; the Bolsheviks machine-gunned monasteries. It is more dangerous in a different way: it dissolves defenses that once existed. It claims to liberate while it suppresses. It colonizes language so that words like "persecution" and "minority" arrive pre-assigned. It offers no limiting principle, then spreads through institutions rather than armies.

You cannot negotiate with HR. You cannot appeal a faculty resolution like a Roman decree. The coercion is bureaucratic, distributed, and self-replicating. It can deny you employment, status, access, platform, and ordinary standing in public life‚Äîwithout ever announcing itself as persecution.

Orthodoxy, in particular, is anomalous to Western categories: too Christian to be exotic, too foreign to be default, too ancient to be legible. Islam is widely recognized as vulnerable even while many Muslims have states abroad. Judaism receives institutional protection through Holocaust memory. Protestantism blends into American civic religion. Orthodoxy fits nowhere. What the framework cannot place, it processes as threat.

Representative Wilson demonstrates the point in real time. He cannot comprehend a church that never pretended faith was a private hobby detachable from peoplehood, memory, and ritual life. He cannot interpret ROCOR's defense of the Ukrainian Orthodox Church except as geopolitical alignment. The possibility that it is defense of ecclesial communion against state interference‚Äîthe very reason ROCOR exists‚Äîdoes not register as an available category.

The Complexity of Russianism

Russianism itself is no less complex than "Whiteness"‚Äîand far more ancient. Where Whiteness emerges as a social construct of colonial modernity, a category invented to justify exploitation and refined through centuries of racial pseudoscience, Russianism is a lived experience spanning over a millennium. It is language, liturgy, and martyrdom. It is the convergence of Slavic, Byzantine, and Mongol influences into a distinct civilization that predates the Enlightenment categories now imposed upon it. To be Russian Orthodox is not to participate in an abstract racial hierarchy but to inherit a specific theological tradition, a particular way of standing before God, a continuity of worship unchanged since before the Great Schism. When my great-grandfather died, he died not for an idea of racial dominance but for a reality‚Äîthe reality of Holy Russia, of the Divine Liturgy, of the Theotokos and all the saints. Racial essentialism flattens this into "White Christian nationalism" and calls it primitive. But which is more reductive: a faith that has maintained the same prayers for a thousand years, or a framework invented in the 1960s that claims to explain all human relations through a checklist?

The modern debate over Russia and Ukraine has further complicated Russianism in American discourse. To express any affinity for Russian Orthodox tradition is to risk accusation of supporting Putin's regime. To advocate for the canonical Ukrainian Orthodox Church under Metropolitan Onuphry‚Äîitself persecuted by the Ukrainian state‚Äîis to be labeled a Kremlin agent. The complexity of ecclesial jurisdiction, the distinction between ROCOR (which has been in exile since 1920) and the Moscow Patriarchate, the reality that Ukrainian and Russian Orthodox share the same liturgy and saints‚Äîall of this is flattened into a binary of aggressor and victim that maps neatly onto racial essentialist frameworks. Russianism becomes not a millennium of lived experience but a political accusation. My great-grandfather's martyrdom becomes suspect. My faith becomes foreign influence.

The Exit

I am not writing to beg for recognition from institutions that operate on this logic. Such recognition would be meaningless even if granted. I am pointing to the exit.

Orthodoxy offers a different anthropology: communion.

Racial essentialism treats persons as positions within power structures. Orthodoxy begins with the Icon: you are made in the Image of God. Human relations are not transactions of guilt, nor negotiations between demographic blocs, but participations in a shared ontological reality. That is not a metaphor. It is what has sustained the Church since 33 AD.

The state could not comprehend it then. It cannot now. It crucified Christ for claiming a Kingdom it could not categorize, and it has tried to crucify His Body ever since‚Äîand failed. The state can kill the body; it cannot touch communion.

Solzhenitsyn captured this plainly: the line between good and evil runs not through states or classes or parties, but through every human heart. That line cannot be mapped onto demographics‚Äînot by Rome, not by the Bolsheviks, not by Representative Wilson, not by any framework that treats the ontological as sociological.

The Church survived Rome, iconoclasts, Mongols, Islam, and Bolshevism. It will survive this too‚Äînot by winning arguments inside materialist systems, but because communion precedes categorization.

I do not need Representative Wilson to stop calling me a foreign agent. I do not need McIntosh's framework to recognize Orthodox persecution. I do not need institutions to update their categories.

I am already free.

We are already free.

We have always been free.

That is what the state cannot see. That is what it has never been able to kill. It's what it will never be able to kill. It's what thinkers like Peggy need to orient themselves towards. And God will do the rest.
