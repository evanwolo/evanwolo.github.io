Critical Analysis

The Kingdom the State Cannot See

When frameworks designed to detect power encounter sacramental communion, they mistake their own blindness for hidden operations.

By Evan Wologodzew · January 15, 2026 · 12 min read

In November 2025, Congressman Joe Wilson accused clergy in the Russian Orthodox Church Outside Russia, including my bishop, of acting as "vehicles of foreign influence" because they defended religious freedom for the persecuted Ukrainian Orthodox Church.

Here is what Wilson said: these men are foreign agents, operating through institutional networks, advancing geopolitical interests under cover of religious authority. The accusation arrived with the full confidence of someone who has identified the obvious. It had the weight of analysis. It sounded like investigation.

It wasn't. It was a category error performing itself as revelation.

"Are you the King of the Jews?" Pilate asked. Christ did not negotiate the premise. "My kingdom is not of this world."

Pilate could not compute an answer that refused political taxonomy. Wilson cannot either. Neither could the Bolsheviks who executed my great-grandfather for loyalty to the Tsar and to the Orthodox faith. They also had sophisticated frameworks for identifying institutional vehicles of power. They also knew how to recognize oppressor classes operating through traditional structures. They also killed with clear conscience, certain they were eliminating threats that had been correctly identified.

The cast changes. The vocabulary updates. The bureaucracy learns new manners. The mechanism stays intact.

When the state cannot see communion, it explains the Church materially. And once the Church is reduced to matter, suppression becomes rational administration.

A Test That Does Not Require Faith

You do not need to accept Orthodox theology to see the pattern. You need only accept this: if two analytical frameworks designed to detect completely different threats—geopolitical subversion and racialized power preservation—produce identical misidentifications when examining the same organization, then at least one of three things is true. Either (1) the organization is simultaneously enacting both forms of hidden power with perfect coordination, or (2) both frameworks are independently correct and the similarity is coincidence, or (3) both frameworks share a structural limitation that produces false positives of the same type. The simpler explanation is (3). The question is what that limitation is. This essay claims the limitation is ontological: frameworks built to measure material power coordination cannot distinguish between coordination through material interests and coordination through non-material bonds. That claim can be tested by examining whether the frameworks consistently mistake the latter for the former—which is what the rest of this essay demonstrates.

The Trap of Legibility

We need not be theological allies to recognize the methodological failure here. In public life, we often reason as practical materialists—even when our private metaphysics are richer. We assume that the "real" is what can be modeled: incentives, institutions, information flows, and power dynamics.

This is a problem of legibility. Institutions—especially states—must simplify the complex world into trackable proxies to govern it. They map funding streams, diagram hierarchies, and track influence operations. Over time, the institution mistakes the proxies for the thing itself. If it cannot be plotted on an org chart or a financial ledger, it is treated as nonexistent.

But when an apparatus designed to track material influence encounters a form of association that is not material, it does not return a "null" result. It returns a confident false positive. Even if you believe only material causes exist, you should expect systematic misreadings when the measuring apparatus is tuned to the wrong variables. Wilson is using a radar system to detect a spirit; the static he is hearing is real, but his interpretation of it as a "hidden stealth aircraft" is a projection of his own tools.

What Wilson Cannot See

Wilson thinks he has identified institutional actors engaged in geopolitical alignment. What he has actually encountered is this: the Body of Christ, sacramentally present, continuing in history through the Eucharist.

Not a metaphor for community. Not organizational structure with spiritual aesthetics. The ontological continuation of the Incarnation in history, made present through the mysteries, uniting the faithful across time and geography in literal communion with the divine nature.

This is the claim. This is what Orthodox ecclesiology states, without apology and without metaphor. The Church is the Body of Christ—not represents, not symbolizes, but is—made present through participation in His body and blood. When the faithful receive the Eucharist, they are incorporated into that Body. When a bishop defends persecuted Orthodox Christians in Ukraine, he is acting not as an ethnic advocate or a geopolitical proxy but as an organic member of a single transnational organism united by sacramental reality.

Wilson is colliding with that.

He has no category for it. His analytical framework gives him words for asset and influence and network and foreign. It has no words for communion and sacrament and theosis and Body. So when he encounters the actual thing, his entire apparatus misfires while still feeling like it is working perfectly.

He is not lying. He is not even stupid. He is trapped in a paradigm that can only process human organization through material power relations. The framework is sophisticated. It can track institutional connections, analyze geopolitical alignment, map transnational networks. It does all of this with precision.

But precision is not accuracy when you are measuring the wrong dimension.

Wilson sees bishops from multiple nations defending a persecuted Church. He sees institutional coordination that crosses national borders. He sees public advocacy that does not align with current American foreign policy. He sees all of this, and every analytical tool he has tells him: foreign influence operation.

What he cannot see: these bishops are united not by nationality or political interest but by the Eucharistic chalice. They are not coordinating strategy. They are expressing the organic unity of a single Body that does not recognize the nation-state as its fundamental organizing principle. They defend the Ukrainian Orthodox Church not because Moscow tells them to, but because you cannot amputate part of the Body and pretend it is not your own flesh being severed.

He has built a sophisticated apparatus for detecting geopolitical influence. He has pointed it at sacramental communion. The readings he is getting are real. But what they are measuring is the consistent failure of his tools to find what they are designed to detect—and he is interpreting that failure as confirmation of a hidden operation.

The vocabulary was foreign influence. The mechanism was reduction. The result was suppression with a congressional seal on it.

States Do Weaponize Churches

This is not contested. Different regimes suppress in different ways; the parallel here is not moral equivalence but a recurring misrecognition—the reduction of communion to administrable power.

Emperors have appointed bishops. Patriarchs have blessed tanks. Clergy have carried water for regimes. The Russian state has attempted to use the Church as a propaganda apparatus. The Soviet state successfully infiltrated it. The Byzantine state treated it as a department of imperial administration. This has happened. This is happening. This will happen again.

The question is not whether churches can be captured. The question is whether capture is what a church is.

Wilson sees clergy defending a persecuted Church across national lines and concludes: geopolitical coordination. He is not imagining a pattern that doesn't exist. States do use religious networks. His error is not empirical. It is ontological. He is treating the possibility of instrumentalization as proof of essence.

This is the move: because the Church can be weaponized, the Church must be essentially a weapon. Because bishops can be co-opted, bishops must be essentially agents. Because communion creates transnational bonds, those bonds must be essentially foreign influence.

But a body can be sick without sickness being what a body is. A marriage can be abused without abuse being what marriage is. And the Church can be infiltrated, corrupted, and weaponized by states without the state's use of it being the Church's reality.

The frameworks Wilson and his progressive mirror-images wield have no category for "distortion of an essence that is not itself political." They can see corruption. They can see capture. What they cannot see is that corruption and capture are parasitic—that they depend on something prior that is not itself explicable as power coordination.

You cannot weaponize what does not exist. If the Church were merely an institutional vehicle for ethnic or geopolitical interest, there would be nothing for the state to capture. The very fact that states attempt to co-opt the Church is evidence that the Church has a reality the state does not control and therefore seeks to redirect.

Sacramental communion is that reality. It precedes the state's attempt to use it. It survives the state's attempt to destroy it. And it continues whether or not any particular bishop, patriarch, or parish has been compromised.

When bishops engage in political advocacy—when they lobby on Capitol Hill, when they organize delegations, when they coordinate public messaging—they are acting in the civic sphere where questions about funding, communications, and institutional ties are entirely legitimate. If Wilson has evidence that bishops received instructions from Russian intelligence, that diocesan funds flowed from sanctioned entities, that advocacy campaigns were coordinated with Kremlin officials, that evidence matters and deserves investigation.

The distinction is not between "spiritual activity" (which can't be questioned) and "political activity" (which can). Bishops defending persecuted Christians is both spiritual and political. It has sacramental roots and civic consequences. The question is not whether such activity should be examined, but how it is interpreted.

Wilson can legitimately investigate coordination, funding, and governance ties. What he cannot do—what his framework prevents him from doing—is distinguish between coordination through sacramental communion and coordination using sacramental language as cover. Both produce similar patterns: transnational advocacy, aligned messaging, institutional cooperation. But they are not the same thing.

Evidence of coordination does not settle which kind of coordination occurred. Documents showing bishops communicated across borders, that advocacy was organized, that messaging aligned—all of this is compatible with either sacramental communion expressing itself politically or geopolitical interests weaponizing ecclesial language. You cannot determine which is happening by examining the coordination itself. You need to know what you're looking at before you can interpret what you're seeing.

This is not a claim that bishops are immune from scrutiny. It is a claim that scrutiny requires categories adequate to what is being scrutinized. If your framework can only detect material power coordination, you will find material power coordination—whether or not that is what's actually unifying the activity you observe.

We have seen this confusion before.

The Ancient Accusation, Updated

The charge itself is ancient. Only the slogans differ.

Rome called Christians politically subversive because we would not worship the Emperor. What appeared to Roman authorities as refusal to recognize legitimate sovereignty was actually refusal to assign ultimate allegiance to anything other than the Kingdom of God. The analytical framework of Roman power could process this only as sedition. It was not that Rome examined the Christian claim and rejected it on philosophical grounds. It was that the Christian claim was illegible within the categories Rome used to maintain order. The framework did not malfunction. It accurately detected something that would not submit to its ordering logic and correctly identified it as requiring suppression.

The vocabulary was political subversion. The mechanism was reduction. The result was lions.

The Arians treated Orthodox confession as dangerous because it would not be adjusted for imperial convenience. When the Council of Nicaea insisted that Christ was homoousios with the Father—of one essence, not merely similar—this was not a minor theological distinction. It was an assertion that ultimate reality could not be negotiated to accommodate political expediency. The Church was saying: we cannot adjust the creed even for the Emperor, because the creed describes what is, not what is diplomatically useful. The Arian position was not theologically incoherent. It was politically manageable. Orthodoxy was not.

The vocabulary was imperial discord. The mechanism was reduction. The result was exile.

The Bolsheviks called Orthodoxy "Great Russian chauvinism" because it was inseparable from a civilization they meant to destroy. They did not misunderstand their target. They understood it perfectly well within their framework: the Church was the ideological apparatus of the imperial order, the spiritual justification for class oppression, the institutional vehicle through which the old regime maintained cultural hegemony. Every analytical tool they had confirmed this reading. When they executed priests, when they dynamited cathedrals, when they sent my great-grandfather to face a firing squad, they did so with intellectual confidence. They had identified the structures of power correctly. They had recognized the enemy accurately.

What they could not see—what their entire framework was built to prevent them from seeing—was that the Church was not primarily any of those things. It was the sacramental presence of the Kingdom of God, continuing in history regardless of which empire claimed dominion, persisting through the collapse of every political order that tried to either weaponize it or destroy it.

The Bolsheviks looked at the liturgy and saw mystification of class relations. They looked at the bishops and saw institutional functionaries of the old regime. They looked at the faithful and saw a peasant class that needed to be liberated from superstition. They looked directly at communion and saw everything except what was actually there.

The vocabulary was class oppression. The mechanism was reduction. The result was mass graves.

The cast changes. The vocabulary updates. The mechanism stays intact.

The Progressive Framework Does The Exact Same Thing

Peggy McIntosh's "invisible knapsack" argues that whiteness functions as an invisible package of unearned advantages. One of the privileges she names is never being asked to speak for one's entire racial group. The framework claims to diagnose collective projection while making the individual invisible.

It has become the moral dialect of Western institutions. HR departments, university administrations, accreditation standards, corporate compliance trainings, grant-making language—the framework saturates the bureaucratic infrastructure. It is not merely opinion. It is the default conscience of organizations that want to manage populations while appearing neutral.

When this framework encounters Orthodoxy, it performs the identical operation as Wilson's geopolitical analysis. It looks at a communion that includes Ethiopian, Antiochian, Russian, Greek, Arab, Romanian, Serbian, and Georgian traditions—united not by race or language but by the Eucharist—and it cannot process multi-ethnic sacramental unity except as camouflaged power.

The framework is built to detect racialized power dynamics. It is very good at this. It can identify patterns of advantage that operate beneath conscious awareness. It can trace how historical structures create durable disparities. It does all of this with real analytical sophistication.

But when it encounters something that is not a racialized power dynamic, it does not register "category error" or "not applicable." It registers "hidden operation of what I am designed to detect."

So Orthodox adherence to traditional liturgy becomes "white Christian nationalism." Orthodox resistance to doctrinal innovation becomes "preservation of patriarchal structures." Orthodox fidelity to creedal teaching becomes "disguised supremacy." The framework sees people defending theological positions that have been held by African, Middle Eastern, and Slavic Christians for two thousand years, and it translates this into: white people protecting white power.

This is not because the people wielding the framework are acting in bad faith. It is because the framework itself cannot see what it is looking at. It has one move: translate all human organization into relations of racialized power. When it encounters organization that does not operate through those relations, the framework does not pause. It intensifies.

Denial becomes evidence. If you say "this is not about race, this is about sacramental unity," the framework hears: you are so deeply embedded in invisible privilege that you cannot see your own complicity. If you cite the multi-ethnic composition of Orthodoxy, the framework responds: you are using diversity as a shield. If you point to the actual content of theological claims, the framework dismisses this as distraction: the real issue is always power, and theology is simply the mystification through which power operates.

The structure is unfalsifiable. Every response that does not accept the framework's categories is interpreted as confirmation of the framework's analysis. Protest becomes proof. Defense becomes confession. Silence becomes admission.

Wilson encounters bishops united by sacramental communion and translates this into foreign coordination. McIntosh's inheritors encounter the faithful united by creedal confession and translate this into racialized solidarity. Both frameworks are sophisticated. Both are confident. Both produce the same category error while interpreting their own confusion as successful detection of camouflage.

The vocabulary was white privilege. The mechanism was reduction. The result was institutional suppression with a diversity statement.

Two Frameworks, One Blindness

Wilson's geopolitical analysis and McIntosh's privilege framework are not secret allies. They emerge from incompatible political traditions. They would diagnose each other as problems. A Cold War conservative and a progressive antiracist do not share a worldview.

But they share an ontology: all human organization is ultimately explicable through material power relations.

This is not a minor methodological assumption. It is a metaphysical claim about what is fundamentally real. Both frameworks assert that when people claim to be united by something non-material—divine grace, sacramental communion, participation in the uncreated energies of God—what is actually happening is material coordination that has been mystified. Scratch the surface of any spiritual claim and you will find the real story: ethnic solidarity, geopolitical alignment, racialized power, institutional self-interest.

The ontology is not argued. It is axiomatic. It is the water these frameworks swim in. And from within that ontology, the Church is literally unintelligible.

Orthodoxy says: There is communion. There is theosis. There is sacramental ontology. There is transfiguration. These are not names for material realities we cannot yet measure. These are realities. They are more fundamental than what you are measuring. The person is not a unit of demographic analysis. The person is an icon made in the Image of God. The faithful are not bound by shared material interests. They are bound by participation in the divine nature through grace. The Church does not persist because it serves geopolitical functions or provides cultural cohesion. It persists because it is the Body of Christ, and the gates of hell cannot prevail against it.

This claim does not fit into frameworks built on the axiom that all organization is material power. It is not that those frameworks have evaluated the claim and found it false. It is that the claim is structured in a way their instruments cannot detect.

So they do what instruments do when they encounter something outside their range: they return the closest reading they are capable of producing. Wilson's framework returns: foreign influence. McIntosh's framework returns: white nationalism. Both are confident. Both feel like revelation. Both are measuring their own limits and reporting the results as discovered facts about the target.

What This Means For The Persecuted

I was baptized into the Russian Orthodox Church Outside Russia as an infant. My great-grandfather was executed by the Bolsheviks for loyalty to the Tsar and to the Orthodox faith. His body was discarded. There is no grave for me to visit. My presence in America is evidence of that annihilation. I am not here because my people conquered new lands. I am here because they fled their own with nothing but liturgy.

I do not cite this to request pity. I cite it because it makes the pattern visible.

The Bolsheviks did not target my family because they were weak. They targeted them because Orthodoxy was perceived as too rooted, too durable, too woven into an imperial order the revolution meant to erase. My great-grandfather did not die as a powerless man. He died as what the regime identified as an oppressor class. The bullet was justified as justice. The execution was performed with the confidence of people who had correctly analyzed the structures of power and were now dismantling them.

Now watch the logic recycle with upgraded vocabulary.

The Bolshevik calls you oppressor and names your elimination righteous. The modern bureaucrat calls you privileged and treats your self-defense as proof. The congressman calls you foreign influence and treats your public speech as subversion.

In every case the Church disappears as Church. Spiritual reality is replaced with political category. Communion is translated into conspiracy. Sacrament becomes strategy. Liturgy becomes messaging. Fidelity becomes faction.

The method is the same. Only the efficiency improves.

The Persecution Is Not Incidental

When Saul was persecuting the Church, Christ did not ask, "Why are you persecuting my followers?" He asked, "Saul, Saul, why are you persecuting me?"

Not the community. Not the individuals. Me.

Orthodox ecclesiology claims the Church is the Body of Christ—sacramentally and mystically present, not organizationally or symbolically. When the state persecutes the Church, it is not merely attacking a religious institution or a community of believers. It is colliding with the ontological reality of the Incarnation continuing in history.

This is why the persecution is diagnostic rather than incidental. The state must misrecognize the Church, because if it recognized the Church as what it actually is—the Body of the incarnate God, continuing in history through the mysteries—the state would have to acknowledge a Kingdom that supersedes its own categories entirely.

So it translates what it cannot see into what it can manage: ethnicity, politics, privilege, foreign influence, institutional self-interest, ideological threat.

Rome could not comprehend a Kingdom not of this world. The Arians could not comprehend doctrine that would not bend for imperial convenience. The Bolsheviks could not comprehend a communion that transcended class. Wilson cannot comprehend a Body united by the Eucharist across national borders. McIntosh's inheritors cannot comprehend multiethnic unity in creedal confession.

The frameworks change. The persecution continues.

And the Church continues, because the state can kill bodies, confiscate buildings, interrogate priests, infiltrate parishes, blacklist donors, ruin careers, and deploy every sophisticated tool of analysis and suppression it possesses.

But it cannot kill a sacrament. It cannot imprison grace. It cannot legislate theosis out of existence.

This is not triumphalism. This is not a claim that the Church is immune to suffering. Millions of Orthodox Christians have been martyred. Entire civilizations have been destroyed. The blood of the faithful has soaked into the ground of every century since Pentecost.

But the Church does not survive despite persecution. The Church is revealed through persecution, because persecution is what happens when the Kingdom of God encounters kingdoms that can only see power. The collision is inevitable. The misrecognition is structural. The violence is predictable.

And through all of it, the liturgy continues. The Eucharist is celebrated. Communion persists. Not because the Church is politically savvy or institutionally resilient, but because what the state is trying to destroy is not actually subject to the state's power.

The state keeps trying. It has been trying for two thousand years. It will continue trying, with whatever analytical frameworks the age provides. And it will continue failing to understand why it cannot succeed.

The Line Through Every Heart

Solzhenitsyn wrote: "The line separating good and evil passes not through states, nor between classes, nor between political parties either, but right through every human heart."

That line cannot be mapped onto demographics. Not by Rome. Not by the Bolsheviks. Not by Peggy McIntosh. Not by Congressman Wilson.

The Church does not divide the world into oppressor and oppressed, colonizer and colonized, privileged and marginalized. It divides the world into fallen and redeemed—but that line goes through everyone. Through the baptized. Through the bishops. Through me. Through Wilson. Through you.

This is not a retreat to "both sides." This is the alternative ontology.

The Church is not a coalition of the righteous. It is a hospital for sinners being transformed into the likeness of God through participation in His Body. Every person who enters has the icon of God stamped on them and the mark of the fall corrupting them. The line runs through each heart, and the work of salvation is not the work of identifying which demographic is on the right side of history. It is the work of being transfigured through grace into the likeness of the One whose Body we receive.

This is what frameworks built on demographic analysis cannot metabolize. They need categories that can be audited. They need groups that can be assessed for relative power. They need systems that can be mapped onto historical patterns of advantage and disadvantage.

Orthodoxy offers an anthropology that does not fit: the person as icon, communion as reality, sin as universal, grace as particular, transformation as the point.

When you try to run demographic analysis on that, the instruments return noise. And the people operating the instruments interpret the noise as evidence of something being hidden.

The Choice

You have now watched two completely different analytical frameworks—Cold War geopolitics and progressive antiracism—make the identical category error about the identical reality.

Not because they are secretly aligned. Because they share the same foundational assumption about what is ultimately real.

You can continue to insist that sacramental ontology is simply mystification of material power. That when Orthodox Christians claim to be united by communion in the Body of Christ, what is actually happening is ethnic solidarity or geopolitical coordination or preservation of privilege, and the spiritual language is just the camouflage.

But if that is true, you need to explain why frameworks designed to detect completely different forms of hidden power—anti-communist frameworks and antiracist frameworks—both produce the same misidentification when pointed at the same liturgical reality. Why does Wilson see foreign influence and McIntosh's framework see white nationalism when both are looking at multitudes unified by the Eucharist?

The simpler explanation is that both frameworks are encountering something they are not built to recognize, and both are interpreting their own inability to find material power relations as evidence that the material power relations are being successfully hidden.

You are free to maintain that everything is ultimately material. But you should at least notice that you are making a metaphysical claim, not performing a neutral analysis. You are asserting an ontology: nothing exists that cannot be explained through power relations. That assertion is not science. It is not investigation. It is a theological position about the nature of reality.

The Church makes a different claim. Not a better analysis of power. A claim about what is real.

There is the Kingdom. There is communion. There is theosis. There is the Body of Christ, present through the mysteries, continuing in history, transfiguring persons into the likeness of God.

The state cannot see this. It never could. When Pilate asked, "What is truth?" he was standing in front of Truth Incarnate and could not recognize it because his framework had no category for it.

Wilson cannot see it. The frameworks saturating Western institutions cannot see it. They will continue to misrecognize the Church as threat, to translate communion into conspiracy, to mistake fidelity for faction. They will do this with sophisticated tools and clear conscience. They will escalate when their tools return confusing readings, certain that confusion is evidence of successful camouflage.

And through all of it, the Church will continue. Not because it is politically strategic. Not because it has powerful allies. Because it is the Body of Christ, and you cannot kill that with administrative action.

The line separating good and evil runs through your heart. Through mine. Through every human heart, regardless of demographic category.

Choose communion.
