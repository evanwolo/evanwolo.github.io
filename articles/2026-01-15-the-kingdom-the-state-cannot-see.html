Critical Analysis

The Kingdom the State Cannot See

When frameworks designed to detect power encounter sacramental communion, they mistake their own blindness for hidden operations.

By Evan Wologodzew · January 15, 2026 · 12 min read

In November 2025, Congressman Joe Wilson accused clergy in the Russian Orthodox Church Outside Russia, including my bishop, of acting as "vehicles of foreign influence" because they defended religious freedom for the persecuted Ukrainian Orthodox Church.

Here is what Wilson said: these men are foreign agents, operating through institutional networks, advancing geopolitical interests under cover of religious authority. The accusation arrived with the full confidence of someone who has identified the obvious. It had the weight of analysis. It sounded like investigation.

It wasn't. It was a category error performing itself as revelation.

"Are you the King of the Jews?" Pilate asked. Christ did not negotiate the premise. "My kingdom is not of this world."

Pilate could not compute an answer that refused political taxonomy. Wilson cannot either. Neither could the Bolsheviks who executed my great-grandfather for loyalty to the Tsar and to the Orthodox faith. They also had sophisticated frameworks for identifying institutional vehicles of power. They also knew how to recognize oppressor classes operating through traditional structures. They also killed with clear conscience, certain they were eliminating threats that had been correctly identified.

The cast changes. The vocabulary updates. The bureaucracy learns new manners. The mechanism stays intact.

When the state cannot see communion, it explains the Church materially. And once the Church is reduced to matter, suppression becomes rational administration.

A Test That Does Not Require Faith

You do not need to accept Orthodox theology to see the pattern.

You need only accept this: if two analytical frameworks designed to detect completely different threats—geopolitical subversion and racialized power preservation—produce identical misidentifications when examining the same organization, then at least one of three things is true.

Either (1) the organization is simultaneously enacting both forms of hidden power with perfect coordination, or (2) both frameworks are independently correct and the similarity is coincidence, or (3) both frameworks share a structural limitation that produces false positives of the same type.

The simpler explanation is (3). The question is what that limitation is.

This essay claims the limitation is ontological: frameworks built to measure material power coordination cannot distinguish between coordination through material interests and coordination through non-material bonds.

That claim can be tested by examining whether the frameworks consistently mistake the latter for the former—which is what the rest of this essay demonstrates.

The Trap of Legibility

We need not be theological allies to recognize the methodological failure here. In public life, we often reason as practical materialists—even when our private metaphysics are richer. We assume that the "real" is what can be modeled: incentives, institutions, information flows, and power dynamics.

This is a problem of legibility. Institutions—especially states—must simplify the complex world into trackable proxies to govern it. They map funding streams, diagram hierarchies, and track influence operations. Over time, the institution mistakes the proxies for the thing itself. If it cannot be plotted on an org chart or a financial ledger, it is treated as nonexistent.

But when an apparatus designed to track material influence encounters a form of association that is not material, it does not return a "null" result. It returns a confident false positive. Even if you believe only material causes exist, you should expect systematic misreadings when the measuring apparatus is tuned to the wrong variables. Wilson is using a radar system to detect a spirit; the static he is hearing is real, but his interpretation of it as a "hidden stealth aircraft" is a projection of his own tools.

What Wilson Cannot See

Wilson thinks he has unearthed a hidden network. What he has actually encountered is the Body of Christ, sacramentally present, continuing in history.

Not a metaphor for community. Not organizational structure with spiritual aesthetics. The ontological continuation of the Incarnation, uniting the faithful across time and geography.

This is the claim. It is what Orthodox ecclesiology states without apology. The Church is the Body of Christ—not represents, not symbolizes, but is. When a bishop defends persecuted Orthodox Christians in Ukraine, he is acting not as an ethnic advocate or a geopolitical proxy, but as an organic member of a single organism who feels the pain of a severed limb.

This claim is not unique in being spiritual. What makes it unusual is that it cannot be translated into materialist categories without destruction. Most Western Christianity has already been metabolized: reduced to personal experience, community formation, moral teaching, social justice work. These can be understood within materialist frameworks because they've been shaped by them. Orthodoxy's claim resists this reduction. The Eucharist is not a symbol, not an experience, not a teaching—it is the Body of Christ. That incompatibility is what creates the collision.

Wilson is colliding with this reality, but he has no sensors to detect it.

His analytical framework gives him words for asset, influence, network, and foreign. It has no words for communion, sacrament, or Body. So when he encounters the actual thing, his apparatus misfires.

He is not lying. He is likely not even acting with malice. He is simply trapped in a paradigm that can only process human organization through material power relations. The framework is not sophisticated; it is merely efficient. It sorts the world into "allies" and "threats" based on material alignment.

But efficiency is not accuracy when you are measuring the wrong dimension.

Wilson sees bishops from multiple nations defending a persecuted Church. He sees institutional coordination that crosses borders. He sees public advocacy that does not align with current foreign policy. He sees all of this, and his tool returns the only reading it is capable of generating: foreign influence operation.

The radar, again. He has pointed it at sacramental communion. The "blips" he is seeing are real, but he is interpreting the signal of a spiritual bond as the signature of a stealth aircraft. He is mistaking the limitations of his own instrument for the discovery of a hidden enemy.

The vocabulary was foreign influence. The mechanism was reduction. The result was suppression with a congressional seal.

States Do Weaponize Churches

This is not contested. Different regimes suppress in different ways; the parallel here is not moral equivalence but a recurring misrecognition—the reduction of communion to administrable power.

Emperors have appointed bishops. Patriarchs have blessed tanks. Clergy have carried water for regimes. The Russian state has attempted to use the Church as a propaganda apparatus. The Soviet state successfully infiltrated it. The Byzantine state treated it as a department of imperial administration. This has happened. This is happening. This will happen again.

The question is not whether churches can be captured. The question is whether capture is what a church is.

Wilson sees clergy defending a persecuted Church across national lines and concludes: geopolitical coordination. He is not imagining a pattern that doesn't exist. States do use religious networks. His error is not empirical. It is ontological. He is treating the possibility of instrumentalization as proof of essence.

This is the move: because the Church can be weaponized, the Church must be essentially a weapon. Because bishops can be co-opted, bishops must be essentially agents. Because communion creates transnational bonds, those bonds must be essentially foreign influence.

But a body can be sick without sickness being what a body is. A marriage can be abused without abuse being what marriage is. And the Church can be infiltrated, corrupted, and weaponized by states without the state's use of it being the Church's reality.

When the Body is Sick: The Moscow Patriarchate and the Hardest Case

This argument must confront its most difficult test: What happens when the sickness is not incidental but systemic? When Patriarch Kirill blesses Russia's invasion of Ukraine, frames the war as metaphysical struggle against "gay pride parades," when hierarchs become indistinguishable from state propaganda—this is not minor distortion. This is profound ecclesial crisis.

The Moscow Patriarchate's alignment with Putin's regime appears to validate the materialist framework entirely. Here is a church structure functioning exactly as state apparatus, using sacramental language as cover for nationalist conquest. The spiritual vocabulary and material coordination are not just entangled—they seem fused.

But the ontological claim matters precisely here: the fact that this is recognizable as betrayal suggests there is something to betray.

If the Church were essentially ethnic solidarity or state apparatus, Kirill's actions would be the Church functioning normally. Yet what we observe instead is mass defections of clergy who recognize this as incompatible with Orthodoxy, Ukrainian Orthodox Christians—including those previously in communion with Moscow—refusing to commemorate Kirill precisely because they understand the Eucharist creates bonds that nationalism violates, the 1872 Council of Constantinople's condemnation of phyletism (the heresy of organizing the Church by ethnicity or nationality) providing theological grounds to call Kirill's position what it is: heresy, and ROCOR itself maintaining critical distance from Moscow Patriarchate positions on the war.

The materialist framework says: "See, the spiritual language was always cover for power." But this reading cannot explain why other Orthodox, using the same theological categories, recognize Kirill's position as corruption. If Orthodoxy were essentially nationalism, there would be no internal grounds for resistance. The resistance arises from people who take sacramental ontology seriously enough to recognize when it is being violated.

How to Distinguish Sacramental Defense from Compromised Structures

Orthodoxy itself provides criteria for making this distinction:

Does the action preserve communion or fracture it? Kirill's blessing of conquest fractures the Body. Defending persecuted believers' right to worship preserves it.

Does it align with conciliar teaching or contradict it? Blessing nationalist aggression contradicts the condemnation of phyletism. Defending religious freedom aligns with consistent Orthodox teaching on the Church's independence from state control.

Does it treat the Church as subordinate to state interests or maintain proper independence? When bishops baptize state policy with theological language, they subordinate the Church. When they defend believers against state persecution—regardless of which state—they maintain independence.

When ROCOR bishops defended the Ukrainian Orthodox Church's right to its buildings and freedom of worship—a Church oppressed by both Russian aggression and Ukrainian state seizure—they acted in defense of persecuted members of the Body (sacramental consistency), opposed both Ukrainian state seizure of churches and Russian military aggression (not aligning with any single state power), and applied the same principle they apply to Copts in Egypt or Orthodox in Kosovo (universality of defense). This is distinguishable from Kirill's position because it does not sanctify state policy. It applies ecclesial principles that often contradict state interests.

The frameworks Wilson and his progressive counterparts wield have no category for "distortion of an essence that is not itself political." They can see corruption. They can see capture. What they cannot see is that corruption and capture are parasitic—that they depend on something prior that is not itself explicable as power coordination.

You cannot weaponize what does not exist. If the Church were merely an institutional vehicle for ethnic or geopolitical interest, there would be nothing for the state to capture. The very fact that states attempt to co-opt the Church is evidence that the Church has a reality the state does not control and therefore seeks to redirect.

Sacramental communion is that reality. It precedes the state's attempt to use it. It survives the state's attempt to destroy it. And it continues whether or not any particular bishop, patriarch, or parish has been compromised.

The Difference Between Scrutiny and Recognition

When bishops engage in political advocacy—when they lobby on Capitol Hill, when they organize delegations, when they coordinate public messaging—they are acting in the civic sphere where questions about funding, communications, and institutional ties are entirely legitimate. If Wilson has evidence that bishops received instructions from Russian intelligence, that diocesan funds flowed from sanctioned entities, that advocacy campaigns were coordinated with Kremlin officials, that evidence matters and deserves investigation.

The distinction is not between "spiritual activity" (which can't be questioned) and "political activity" (which can). Bishops defending persecuted Christians is both spiritual and political. It has sacramental roots and civic consequences. The question is not whether such activity should be examined, but how it is interpreted.

Wilson can legitimately investigate coordination, funding, and governance ties. What he cannot do—what his framework prevents him from doing—is distinguish between coordination through sacramental communion and coordination using sacramental language as cover. Both produce similar patterns: transnational advocacy, aligned messaging, institutional cooperation. But they are not the same thing.

Evidence of coordination does not settle which kind of coordination occurred. Documents showing bishops communicated across borders, that advocacy was organized, that messaging aligned—all of this is compatible with either sacramental communion expressing itself politically or geopolitical interests weaponizing ecclesial language. You cannot determine which is happening by examining the coordination itself. You need to know what you're looking at before you can interpret what you're seeing.

This is not a claim that bishops are immune from scrutiny. It is a claim that scrutiny requires categories adequate to what is being scrutinized. If your framework can only detect material power coordination, you will find material power coordination—whether or not that is what's actually unifying the activity you observe.

We have seen this confusion before.

The Ancient Accusation, Updated

The charge itself is ancient. Only the slogans differ.

Rome called Christians politically subversive because we would not worship the Emperor. What appeared to Roman authorities as refusal to recognize legitimate sovereignty was actually refusal to assign ultimate allegiance to anything other than the Kingdom of God. The analytical framework of Roman power could process this only as sedition. It was not that Rome examined the Christian claim and rejected it on philosophical grounds. It was that the Christian claim was illegible within the categories Rome used to maintain order. The framework did not malfunction. It accurately detected something that would not submit to its ordering logic and correctly identified it as requiring suppression.

The vocabulary was political subversion. The mechanism was reduction. The result was lions.

The Arians treated Orthodox confession as dangerous because it would not be adjusted for imperial convenience. When the Council of Nicaea insisted that Christ was homoousios with the Father—of one essence, not merely similar—this was not a minor theological distinction. It was an assertion that ultimate reality could not be negotiated to accommodate political expediency. The Church was saying: we cannot adjust the creed even for the Emperor, because the creed describes what is, not what is diplomatically useful. The Arian position was not theologically incoherent. It was politically manageable. Orthodoxy was not.

The vocabulary was imperial discord. The mechanism was reduction. The result was exile.

The Bolsheviks called Orthodoxy "Great Russian chauvinism" because it was inseparable from a civilization they meant to destroy. They did not misunderstand their target. They understood it perfectly well within their framework: the Church was the ideological apparatus of the imperial order, the spiritual justification for class oppression, the institutional vehicle through which the old regime maintained cultural hegemony. Every analytical tool they had confirmed this reading. When they executed priests, when they dynamited cathedrals, when they sent my great-grandfather to face a firing squad, they did so with intellectual confidence. They had identified the structures of power correctly. They had recognized the enemy accurately.

What they could not see—what their entire framework was built to prevent them from seeing—was that the Church was not primarily any of those things. It was the sacramental presence of the Kingdom of God, continuing in history regardless of which empire claimed dominion, persisting through the collapse of every political order that tried to either weaponize it or destroy it.

The Bolsheviks looked at the liturgy and saw mystification of class relations. They looked at the bishops and saw institutional functionaries of the old regime. They looked at the faithful and saw a peasant class that needed to be liberated from superstition. They looked directly at communion and saw everything except what was actually there.

The vocabulary was class oppression. The mechanism was reduction. The result was mass graves.

The cast changes. The vocabulary updates. The mechanism stays intact.

Two Frameworks, One Blindness

Wilson's geopolitical analysis and McIntosh's privilege framework are not secret allies. They emerge from incompatible political traditions. They would diagnose each other as problems. A Cold War conservative and a progressive antiracist do not share a worldview.

Yet they converge on the same ontological shore: the conviction that all human organization is ultimately explicable through material power relations—whether those relations are cast in terms of state interests, funding streams, and transnational networks, or in terms of unearned advantages, historical legacies, and invisible hierarchies of privilege.

This is not a minor methodological assumption. It is a metaphysical claim about what is fundamentally real. Both frameworks assert that when people claim to be united by something non-material—divine grace, sacramental communion, participation in the uncreated energies of God—what is actually happening is material coordination that has been mystified. Scratch the surface of any spiritual claim, and you will find the real story: ethnic solidarity, geopolitical alignment, racialized power, institutional self-interest.

The ontology is not argued. It is axiomatic. It is the water these frameworks swim in. And from within that ontology, the Church is literally unintelligible.

The Progressive Framework Does The Exact Same Thing

Nowhere is this convergence more evident than when the same materialist gaze turns inward, toward the cultural and racial dynamics of American society. Peggy McIntosh's concept of the "invisible knapsack"—privilege as a package of unearned assets—has become the moral dialect of modern institutions: the default logic for HR departments, universities, corporations, and even some religious bodies that seek to manage populations while appearing neutral.

When this framework encounters Orthodoxy, it performs the identical reduction as Wilson's geopolitical analysis. It looks at a communion that spans Ethiopian, Antiochian, Russian, Greek, Arab, Romanian, Serbian, and Georgian traditions—united not by race, language, or shared historical privilege, but by the Eucharist—and it cannot process this multi-ethnic, transnational sacramental unity except as camouflaged power.

The framework is built to detect racialized power dynamics. It excels at identifying patterns of advantage that operate beneath conscious awareness, tracing how historical structures create durable disparities. These are real insights in many contexts.

But when it encounters an ecclesial reality that refuses racial categorization as its organizing principle—one that has, since the condemnation of phyletism (ethno-racialism) at the 1872 Council of Constantinople, explicitly rejected the idea that the Church should be divided or defined by ethnicity or race—the framework does not register "category mismatch" or "not applicable." Instead, it registers "hidden operation."

Thus, Orthodox adherence to traditional liturgy becomes coded as "white Christian nationalism." Fidelity to ancient creedal teaching becomes "preservation of patriarchal structures." The defense of theological positions held unbroken by African, Middle Eastern, and Slavic Christians for two millennia is reframed as white people protecting white power.

This misreading is not born of bad faith on the part of those who apply the framework. It arises because the framework is a monotone: it has one setting, translating all human organization into relations of privilege and power. When it meets an organization that operates through sacramental bonds rather than racialized ones, the framework does not pause to recalibrate. It overwrites.

Denial becomes evidence. If you say, "This is not about race; this is about sacramental unity," the framework hears: "You are blind to your own invisible knapsack." If you point to the multi-ethnic composition of Orthodoxy—from Coptic martyrs in Egypt to Georgian saints in the Caucasus—the framework responds: "You are deploying performative diversity as a shield."

The structure is unfalsifiable. Every response that refuses its categories is interpreted as confirmation of them.

Wilson encounters bishops united by the Eucharistic chalice and translates this into foreign coordination. The progressive framework encounters the faithful united by the creed and translates this into racialized solidarity. Both are confident. Both feel like revelation. Both are, in truth, the mechanical failure of a materialist worldview attempting to read a spiritual map.

The vocabulary was white privilege. The mechanism was reduction. The result was institutional suppression with a diversity statement.

What This Means For The Persecuted

When my grandfather was 15, my great-grandfather was taken—kidnapped by the state, executed by firing squad for refusing to renounce his fidelity to the Tsar as God's anointed and to the Orthodox faith that bound them. His body was discarded, denied even the dignity of a grave. There is no marker, no place to lay flowers or pray at his resting place. That absence is itself an inflicted multi-generational wound, a deliberate attempt to obliterate memory.

Yet memory endures through the stories we tell, through the unbroken chain of liturgy that carried survivors into exile. I was baptized into the Russian Orthodox Church Outside Russia as an infant. ROCOR is the same church that allowed me to venerate not only that legacy, but the legacy of my grandfather who was afforded the funeral his father was denied.

The Bolsheviks targeted us not because we were weak, but because Orthodoxy was too rooted, too durable, too inseparable from a civilization they meant to erase. My great-grandfather did not die as a powerless man. He died classified as an oppressor class, his execution framed as justice. The bullet was justified by the very analytical confidence that now recurs in new vocabularies.

My presence in America is a direct product of that annihilation. My last name, my heritage, was expelled from my motherland by force—not through conquest or choice, but through state terror that sought to erase loyalty to Christ's anointed and to the Church. Here, the same Eucharist is still celebrated in parishes of the Russian Orthodox Church Outside Russia—such as the Protection of the Mother of God Church in Rochester, NY—preserving what the regime tried to destroy and bearing witness to the resilience of communion that no firing squad could sever.

Now watch the logic recycle. The Bolshevik called you oppressor and named your elimination righteous. The modern bureaucrat calls you privileged and treats your self-defense as proof. The congressman calls you foreign influence and treats your public speech as subversion.

In every case the Church disappears as Church. Spiritual reality is replaced with political category. Communion is translated into conspiracy. Sacrament becomes strategy. Liturgy becomes messaging. Fidelity becomes faction.

The method is the same. Only the efficiency improves.

The Persecution Is Not Incidental

When Saul was persecuting the Church, Christ did not ask, "Why are you persecuting my followers?" He asked, "Saul, Saul, why are you persecuting me?"

Not the community. Not the individuals. Me.

Orthodox ecclesiology claims the Church is the Body of Christ—sacramentally and mystically present, not organizationally or symbolically. When the state persecutes the Church, it is not merely attacking a religious institution or a community of believers. It is colliding with the ontological reality of the Incarnation continuing in history.

This is why the persecution is diagnostic rather than incidental. The state must misrecognize the Church, because if it recognized the Church as what it actually is—the Body of the incarnate God, continuing in history through the mysteries—the state would have to acknowledge a Kingdom that supersedes its own categories entirely.

So it translates what it cannot see into what it can manage: ethnicity, politics, privilege, foreign influence, institutional self-interest, ideological threat.

Rome could not comprehend a Kingdom not of this world. The Arians could not comprehend doctrine that would not bend for imperial convenience. The Bolsheviks could not comprehend a communion that transcended class. Wilson cannot comprehend a Body united by the Eucharist across national borders. McIntosh's inheritors cannot comprehend multiethnic unity in creedal confession.

The frameworks change. The persecution continues.

And the Church continues, because the state can kill bodies, confiscate buildings, interrogate priests, infiltrate parishes, blacklist donors, ruin careers, and deploy every sophisticated tool of analysis and suppression it possesses.

But it cannot kill a sacrament. It cannot imprison grace. It cannot legislate theosis out of existence.

This is not triumphalism. This is not a claim that the Church is immune to suffering. Millions of Orthodox Christians have been martyred. Entire civilizations have been destroyed. The blood of the faithful has soaked into the ground of every century since Pentecost.

But the Church does not survive despite persecution. The Church is revealed through persecution, because persecution is what happens when the Kingdom of God encounters kingdoms that can only see power. The collision is inevitable. The misrecognition is structural. The violence is predictable.

And through all of it, the liturgy continues. The Eucharist is celebrated. Communion persists. Not because the Church is politically savvy or institutionally resilient, but because what the state is trying to destroy is not actually subject to the state's power.

The state keeps trying. It has been trying for two thousand years. It will continue trying, with whatever analytical frameworks the age provides. And it will continue failing to understand why it cannot succeed.

The Line Through Every Heart

Solzhenitsyn wrote: "The line separating good and evil passes not through states, nor between classes, nor between political parties either, but right through every human heart."

That line cannot be mapped onto demographics, functions, or datasets.

Not by the rabbinic authorities who labeled Christ a criminal and delivered Him to Rome, certain they were excising blasphemy from Israel. Not by Rome, which crucified Him as a threat to order, translating Truth Incarnate into a public-security problem.

Not by the Latins who sacked Constantinople, mistaking schism for evil and plunder for righteousness, nor by the Turks who exploited that fracture and read civilizational weakness as permission.

Not by revolutionary France, which did not initially seek to destroy the Church but to domesticate it—recasting priests as state employees, demanding oaths of civic loyalty, and labeling those who refused allegiance to an unseen King as enemies of the people.

Not by the architects of Social Darwinism and eugenics, who relocated evil into biology itself and treated the disabled, the weak, and the unwanted as threats to human progress, baffled by a Church that insisted the Image of God persists precisely where productivity fails.

Not by the Bolsheviks, who drew the line through class and called liquidation liberation.

And not by modern bureaucratic frameworks that draw it through race, privilege, geopolitics, or network graphs—through "whiteness," "foreign influence," or anomalous metadata—and then congratulate themselves for having rendered moral judgment legible to policy and algorithm alike.

In every age the same move is made: evil is externalized, displaced from the human heart into a category that can be administered, corrected, or eliminated.

The Church refuses this relocation. It insists that the line separating good and evil runs through every human heart, including its own—that it is, at once, a spotless Bride and a body with a dirty face.

That refusal is why the Church is always accused of subversion: it will not allow the state to mistake evidence of human failure for the essence of what it is, nor permit the Kingdom of God to be reduced to something the apparatus can finally read, manage, or destroy.

The Church does not divide the world into oppressor and oppressed, colonizer and colonized, privileged and marginalized. It divides the world into fallen and redeemed—but that line goes through everyone. Through the baptized. Through the bishops. Through me. Through Wilson. Through you.

This is not a retreat to "both sides." This is the alternative ontology.

The Church is not a coalition of the righteous. It is a hospital for sinners being transformed into the likeness of God through participation in His Body. Every person who enters has the icon of God stamped on them and the mark of the fall corrupting them. The line runs through each heart, and the work of salvation is not the work of identifying which demographic is on the right side of history. It is the work of being transfigured through grace into the likeness of the One whose Body we receive.

This is what frameworks built on demographic analysis cannot metabolize. They need categories that can be audited. They need groups that can be assessed for relative power. They need systems that can be mapped onto historical patterns of advantage and disadvantage.

Orthodoxy offers an anthropology that does not fit: the person as icon, communion as reality, sin as universal, grace as particular, transformation as the point.

When you try to run demographic analysis on that, the instruments return noise. And the people operating the instruments interpret the noise as evidence of something being hidden.

Rendering Unto Caesar Without Surrendering God

This diagnosis of blindness is not a demand for the state to become theological. We do not expect Congress to legislate based on the Council of Chalcedon. We do not ask the intelligence community to factor the Holy Spirit into their threat assessments.

However, while the state cannot see the Spirit, the Church need not be invisible to the state.

If the collision is inevitable because the state can only measure material risk, then the Church can—and must—speak the language of material evidence, not to validate the state's reductionism, but to exhaust it.

This is the bridge between the radar and the reality. It requires three specific acts of translation.

First: Radical Material Transparency. If the state's only tool is the ledger, then let the ledger be impeccable. When the state suspects that "spiritual bonds" are merely a mask for "illicit funding," our response must be more than indignation; it must be disclosure. We do not fear the audit; we invite it.

In doing so, we offer a striking contrast. Anyone who examines the modern state's own ledger—its staggering debt, its unaccountable flows, its systemic waste—will find a material reality that, by any honest standard, simply does not look good. When the Church opens its books to prove that no foreign ministry underwrites its defense of the persecuted, it forces the state's framework to return a "null result" on the only variable it is capable of processing: money.

Transparency here is not capitulation; it is a form of apophatic witness. It is a demonstration of sacramental order to a bureaucracy drowning in its own material entropy.

Second: The Consistency of the Cross. A geopolitical asset defends only the interests of its patron. A Church defends the Body of Christ wherever it bleeds.

If our defense of the persecuted is truly sacramental, it will be universal. We will defend the Ukrainian Orthodox believers deprived of their churches with the same fervor we defend Coptic Christians in Egypt, Serbian Orthodox in Kosovo, or Antiochian Orthodox in Syria. The bridge is built when the state looks for a pattern of "Russian Foreign Policy" and finds instead a pattern of "Christian Suffering." The former is a threat; the latter is a civic right. Consistency is the evidence that our loyalty is to the Cross, not the Kremlin.

Third: Acknowledging Local Agency. The "foreign influence" model assumes a top-down command structure: a call comes from Moscow, and a bishop in New York obeys. This ignores the Orthodox reality of conciliarity (sobornost). Our bishops are not branch managers of a foreign corporation; they are local shepherds of local flocks.

The Church in America is not an outpost; it is a home. Its members are citizens. Its decisions are made in council, not by decree. By demonstrating that our governance is local—that we possess agency, that we deliberate, that we disagree—we dismantle the caricature of the "sleeper cell." We show the state that we are not a remote-controlled drone, but a living community rooted in the soil where we stand.

We can render unto Caesar the assurance that we are not subversives, without rendering unto him the confession that our communion is merely politics. We can speak to the state in the language of civic order—rights, transparency, independence—while refusing to let the state define who we are.

Two Possible Outcomes

But we must be honest about what transparency can and cannot accomplish. There are two possible outcomes to this approach:

Outcome One: The Confusion is Genuine. In cases where the state truly mistakes sacramental bonds for covert coordination, transparency can demonstrate the difference. The books are open. The funding is domestic. The governance is local. The advocacy follows a pattern of defending persecuted Christians regardless of which state persecutes them. The radar returns null on all material indicators of foreign control. The accusation fails because there is nothing there to find.

Outcome Two: The Recognition is Real. In cases where the state recognizes the Church as a rival source of ultimate allegiance and sees this as inherently threatening, no amount of transparency will satisfy. The ledger can be impeccable, the governance demonstrably local, the consistency of principle evident—and the persecution continues anyway.

Because what threatens the state in this scenario is not hidden material coordination. It is visible spiritual communion that will not submit to comprehensive state authority.

Rome did not misunderstand Christian refusal to worship the Emperor. Rome understood it perfectly and correctly identified it as incompatible with Roman sovereignty. The Arian emperors did not misunderstand that Nicene orthodoxy could not be adjusted for political convenience. They grasped the challenge and responded with exile. The Bolsheviks did not misunderstand that Orthodoxy represented an alternative civilization. They understood and sought to destroy it.

If Wilson—or the bureaucratic apparatus he represents—grasps that bishops who defend persecuted believers across borders are demonstrating allegiance to a Kingdom that supersedes American foreign policy interests, his response may not be confusion. It may be the rational response of a state that recognizes it cannot fully control or co-opt this reality.

The transparency proposal does not naively assume good faith. It clarifies what is actually at stake.

If it is Outcome One, transparency resolves the misunderstanding. The state discovers there is no stealth aircraft, only a mystery it cannot explain.

If it is Outcome Two, transparency ensures that when conflict comes, it comes for the right reasons. We clear away the pretexts—foreign funding, covert coordination, hidden agendas—so that if the state still moves against us, both sides understand what is actually happening: the state is persecuting the Church not because it is a foreign asset, but because it is the Body of Christ.

That is a harder claim. It means accepting that faithfulness may guarantee persecution rather than prevent it. It means recognizing that the collision between the Kingdom of God and kingdoms that can only see power is not always the result of misunderstanding. Sometimes it is the result of understanding perfectly well what cannot be tolerated.

But it is more honest than suggesting transparency will neutralize all threat. The "radar" may never be able to see the Spirit. But if we clear the air of legitimate suspicions about material coordination, even the radar operator will eventually have to admit: there is no stealth aircraft here. There is only a mystery he cannot explain—and must decide whether to accept or destroy.

The Choice

You have now watched two completely different analytical frameworks—Cold War geopolitics and progressive antiracism—make the identical category error about the identical reality.

Not because they are secretly aligned. Because they share the same foundational assumption about what is ultimately real.

You can continue to insist that sacramental ontology is simply mystification of material power. That when Orthodox Christians claim to be united by communion in the Body of Christ, what is actually happening is ethnic solidarity or geopolitical coordination or preservation of privilege, and the spiritual language is just the camouflage.

But if that is true, you need to explain why frameworks designed to detect completely different forms of hidden power—anti-communist frameworks and antiracist frameworks—both produce the same misidentification when pointed at the same liturgical reality. Why does Wilson see foreign influence and McIntosh's framework see white nationalism when both are looking at multitudes unified by the Eucharist?

The simpler explanation is that both frameworks are encountering something they are not built to recognize, and both are interpreting their own inability to find material power relations as evidence that the material power relations are being successfully hidden.

You are free to maintain that everything is ultimately material. But you should at least notice that you are making a metaphysical claim, not performing a neutral analysis. You are asserting an ontology: nothing exists that cannot be explained through power relations. That assertion is not science. It is not investigation. It is a theological position about the nature of reality.

The Church makes a different claim. Not a better analysis of power. A claim about what is real.

There is the Kingdom. There is communion. There is theosis. There is the Body of Christ, present through the mysteries, continuing in history, transfiguring persons into the likeness of God.

The state cannot see this. It never could. When Pilate asked, "What is truth?" he was standing in front of Truth Incarnate and could not recognize it because his framework had no category for it.

Wilson cannot see it. The frameworks saturating Western institutions cannot see it. They will continue to misrecognize the Church as threat, to translate communion into conspiracy, to mistake fidelity for faction. They will do this with administrative tools and clear conscience. They will escalate when their tools return confusing readings, certain that confusion is evidence of successful camouflage.

And through all of it, the Church will continue. Not because it is politically strategic. Not because it has powerful allies. Because it is the Body of Christ, and you cannot kill that with administrative action.

The line separating good and evil runs through your heart. Through mine. Through every human heart, regardless of demographic category.

You do not choose where this line is drawn. You choose which side of it you stand on. This is your obligation.
